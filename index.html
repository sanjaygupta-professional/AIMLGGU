Example: Logistic Regression for Credit Card Default Prediction

‚ùå Weak Example

Logistic Regression is a machine learning algorithm used for classification. In our bank, we can use it to predict which customers will default on their credit cards. We will collect lots of data and train the model. If the accuracy is high, the model is good and we can use it to approve or reject customers. Logistic Regression is good because it is simple and used a lot in the industry. We will check the confusion matrix and try to reduce errors. This will help the bank reduce risk.

Issues:

  • Very generic; could be copied from anywhere
  • No specific mention of data used
  • Relies only on "accuracy" without discussing class imbalance
  • No explicit link to business KPIs
  • No discussion of regulatory or interpretability requirements
  • No mention of validation approach or bias-variance tradeoff
‚úì Strong Example

Business problem: The bank wants to reduce credit card defaults in its mass retail segment without unnecessarily rejecting profitable customers. We frame this as a binary classification problem: default within 12 months (yes/no).

Data: 3 years of historical credit card data including demographics, account behavior (utilization ratio, late payments), and bureau variables. Target label is 90+ days past due within 12 months.

Why Logistic Regression: Risk team and regulators require an interpretable model. Logistic Regression provides clear coefficients and odds ratios. Less prone to overfitting with proper regularization.

KPIs and metrics: Focus on reducing default rate (NPL%) and expected loss. Overall accuracy less meaningful (only 5-7% default rate). Focus on recall, precision, and ROC curve. Select threshold based on risk appetite and misclassification costs.

Validation: Train/validation/test splits with stratified k-fold cross-validation. L2 regularization to control variance. Track performance over time to detect drift.

Risks: Assumes linear relationship in log-odds space. Address with interaction terms. Check for bias across protected groups. Regular back-testing required.

Strengths:

  • Clearly states business problem with specific scope
  • Describes data in realistic detail
  • Explains algorithm appropriateness (interpretability, regulation)
  • Links to concrete business KPIs
  • Proper validation strategy and overfitting control
  • Addresses governance and fairness concerns
